Follow-up line · Internal

Follow-up operator manual.

Follow-up owns the post-signing customer relationship. When Sales hands over a freshly-signed customer, Follow-up becomes their single point of contact — for the life of the loan, often 10–20 years. This manual covers all three tiers and the daily, weekly, and seasonal flows: retention calls, hardship handling, satisfaction surveys, restructure conversations, payoff support.

01 · About the role

Who Follow-up is, and what each tier owns.

Follow-up is the third internal line. The signature pattern: one follow-up agent per customer, for the whole loan. Continuity matters here more than anywhere else in the org — every interaction goes on the timeline so context survives when an agent moves on.

Agent

Follow-up agent

Owns a portfolio of signed customers. Runs the contact cadence (welcome, check-ins, anniversary, hardship calls), works the watch/delinquent list, fields routine portal questions, files deferral requests. The customer's named human contact.

Owns: their customers' experience. Sees: their portfolio, payment status (read-only), document history, full timeline.

Manager

Follow-up manager

Runs a team of follow-up agents. Owns retention metrics, satisfaction scores, and the hardship escalation queue. Reassigns customers when an agent leaves; approves out-of-policy deferrals before they go to Finance.

Owns: the team's retention. Sees: everything an agent sees + team rollup + retention dashboard + escalation queues.

Admin

Follow-up admin

Owns the retention surface — contact cadence policy, satisfaction survey design, hardship playbooks, agent capacity model. Coordinates with Finance admin on deferral / restructure / write-off thresholds.

Owns: the function. Sees: all customers, all teams, all configuration. Cannot edit the loan ledger (Finance) or invite users (Org admin).

03 · Daily flows

What Follow-up does, end-to-end.

Seven flows cover the cadence — from welcoming a fresh handover to negotiating a restructure with a customer in trouble.

01
Receive a handover from Sales
15 min · Within 48h of signing

You'll get a notification when Sales books a new loan and assigns you. The handover summary is the most important document — read it before you call.

  1. Open the handover notification
    Links to the customer detail page.
  2. Read the handover summary
    Three lines: who they are, what closed the deal, what they're sensitive about.
  3. Scroll the timeline
    Catch up on the full pre-signing context — calls, visits, the offer back-and-forth.
  4. Send welcome message
    Use the "Welcome from Follow-up" template. Introduces you by name.
  5. Schedule the 30-day check-in
    Set a task. Auto-included in your cadence but pin it.
02
Run the watch list (Agent)
Daily · 30 min

Customers transition into Watch the day after their installment is missed. Catching them early — before they progress to Delinquent — is the highest-leverage thing Follow-up does.

  1. Customers → Watch
    Filter pre-applied. Sorted by days-since-due descending.
  2. Call each one
    Empathy first, then ask. Don't lead with consequences.
  3. Log outcome
    Connected → outcome; No answer → schedule retry; Promised pay → set follow-up.
  4. File deferral if appropriate
    If hardship is real, start the deferral flow (flow 04).
Three-no-answer rule

After three unanswered call attempts on different days, escalate to your manager. Don't keep dialing without a different tactic — try SMS, then email, then a registered letter via Documents.

03
Answer a portal message
Throughout day · 2–10 min each

Customers DM you from their portal. SLA varies by org but same-day response is standard.

  1. Open the message
    Notification or sidebar → Messages.
  2. Identify the ask
    Routine question → answer in-thread. Payment-posting question → route to Finance. Hardship → start the deferral flow.
  3. Reply
    Use email/message templates where they fit. Always sign with your name.
04
File a deferral
15 min

When a customer can't pay a specific installment. Deferral moves that one installment's due date forward; the rest of the schedule is untouched.

  1. Customer detail → Actions → Deferral
    Pick the installment(s) to defer, new due date, reason code.
  2. Add narrative note
    Free text: what the customer said. Survives forever on the timeline.
  3. Submit for approval
    Manager review if within agent guardrails; admin if out of policy.
  4. Generate deferral letter
    Auto-generated from template; customer e-signs in portal.
  5. Confirm with customer
    Call or message once the new schedule posts. Don't leave them wondering.
05
Negotiate a restructure (Manager)
60+ min · Multi-session

When a customer's circumstances have changed permanently — job loss, household change, income reduction — a deferral isn't enough. Restructure recomputes the whole schedule.

  1. Schedule a hardship call
    Block 45 minutes. Often emotional; not a sales call.
  2. Gather updated financials
    Document request for current income/expenses. Customer uploads.
  3. Run restructure scenarios
    Customer detail → Actions → Restructure. Try extending term, reducing rate, both.
  4. Bring Finance into the loop
    Restructure needs Finance manager + Finance admin sign-off.
  5. Sign the new schedule
    Generate addendum to loan agreement; customer e-signs.
06
Support a payoff request
20 min

Customer asks to pay off. Your role: confirm intent, route the quote request to Finance, walk them through the wire instructions, celebrate the close.

  1. Confirm intent on a call
    Sometimes payoff is exploratory. Don't pull Finance until the customer is serious.
  2. Trigger the quote
    Customer detail → Request payoff quote. Routes to Finance.
  3. Send wire instructions
    From the generated quote PDF, automatically emailed.
  4. Send graduation message
    When payoff posts, send the "You're paid off" template + thank-you. Refer them to refer others.
07
Run satisfaction survey review (Manager)
Weekly · 45 min

Surveys go out automatically at 30 days post-handover, then yearly. Manager reviews the responses and coaches agents on patterns.

  1. Reports → Satisfaction
    Filter by date range, agent, score.
  2. Read the low-scorers
    Negative comments are the actionable ones. Identify common themes.
  3. 1:1 coaching
    Bring specific examples to agent 1:1s. Always assume goodwill; specificity helps.
04 · Contact cadence

The rhythm.

Your default contact cadence per customer. Configured by Follow-up admin; agent executes.

  • Day 0 (signing) — Welcome message from Follow-up agent.
  • Day 30 — Check-in call. "How's the first month been?"
  • Day 90 — Satisfaction survey + status check.
  • Day 180 — Light-touch SMS check-in.
  • Annually — Phone call + relationship review.
  • Anniversary of signing — Personal note.
  • When Watch lifecycle triggers — Same-day or next-day call.
  • When portal message arrives — Same-day response.

08 · FAQ & gotchas

Things that trip people up.

A customer says they were promised something by their Sales agent.

Check the timeline — every promise should be logged. If it's documented, honor it (escalate to manager if it exceeds policy). If undocumented and unreasonable, gently redirect; if undocumented and small, often easier to honor and coach Sales separately.

The customer wants to talk to Finance directly.

You're the front door. For payment-posting questions you can usually answer (read-only access to the ledger). For allocation/reversal questions, loop Finance via the customer detail page — they reply in-thread.

A customer asks for multiple deferrals in a year.

Once is normal. Twice — flag for manager review. Three+ — that's a restructure conversation, not more deferrals. Be honest with the customer about the difference.

Customer is going through divorce, both parties on the loan.

Don't take sides. Document everything in writing. Loop in Org admin — there's usually a legal flag to set on the account so neither party can act unilaterally.

I inherited a customer with no handover notes.

Read the timeline cover-to-cover before your first call. Open the offer to see what they bought and why. Acknowledge on the call that you're new — "I'm getting up to speed on your account" is honest and lands well.

A customer says they can't reach me. But my logs show I've tried.

SMS your number from a logged activity so they have it in writing. Add a calendar link to your email signature. Confirm their preferred contact channel and switch — sometimes a customer who screens calls reads every WhatsApp.

What if a customer needs payment plan but won't pick up the phone?

Document outreach attempts (call → SMS → email → registered letter). After three of each, escalate to manager. Persistence is required but futility isn't — manager may decide to engage a different agent or escalate to legal.
